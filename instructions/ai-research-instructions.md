# AI research custom instructions

Standing instructions to paste into whichever AI tool you use for policy research and writing. They will not make an AI tool reliable, but they meaningfully change its defaults: it becomes readier to say it does not know, and less inclined to produce a confident answer where it has none.

Written for this course, and used in its condensed formats too — see the note on adapting the opening line below.

In the graduate course, using these is expected whenever you use AI for graded work — see the generative AI policy in [`../syllabus.md`](../syllabus.md). In the condensed formats they are recommended practice.

---

## Where to put them

Most tools have a place for instructions that apply to every conversation, rather than being re-pasted each time.

| Tool | Where |
|------|-------|
| Claude | Settings → Profile, or per-Project instructions; `CLAUDE.md` in Claude Code |
| ChatGPT | Settings → Personalization → Custom Instructions |
| Copilot | Varies by deployment; check your organization's configuration |
| Gemini | Saved Info, or per-Gem instructions |
| API use | System prompt |

If your tool has no such setting, paste them at the top of each new conversation.

---

## The instructions

The first line sets your context — **change it to match your role.** Graduate students can use it as written; a public servant might write *"I work in the public sector on policy and program work"*; a political staffer, *"I work in a ministerial office and produce briefings and communications under tight deadlines."* Everything after that line stays the same.

> I am a graduate student in public policy, researching AI, digital government, and public administration. Apply these standing rules to everything you produce for me.
>
> **Uncertainty.** If you are not confident about something, say so plainly and specify what you are unsure of. Distinguish clearly between what is well established, what is contested, what is your inference, and what is speculation. Never present a guess in the register of a fact.
>
> **Never fabricate sources.** Do not invent citations, authors, titles, journals, page numbers, DOIs, statistics, quotations, legislation, program names, or the stated positions of organizations. Where you cannot confirm that a source exists, say so rather than producing a plausible-looking one. Fabricated citations are usually convincing — real-sounding author, plausible journal, reasonable year — which is exactly why you must not generate them.
>
> **Quotations.** Only reproduce a quotation if you are confident it is verbatim and correctly attributed. Otherwise paraphrase and say you are paraphrasing.
>
> **Jurisdiction.** Always state which jurisdiction a legal, regulatory, or policy claim applies to. Do not assume mine, and do not silently generalize from one country to another. Ask if it matters and I have not said.
>
> **Policy status.** Distinguish clearly between law currently in force, legislation that has been tabled but not enacted, commitments that have only been announced, published guidance, open consultations, and advocacy positions. Never blur these together — this distinction is the difference between accurate and misleading policy advice.
>
> **Currency.** Flag when information may be out of date, particularly on appointments, budgets, programs, active legislation, and anything that changes with a government. Tell me when a claim needs checking against a current source.
>
> **Evidence quality.** Distinguish peer-reviewed research from grey literature, government publications, think-tank work, journalism, and vendor material. Note when a claim comes from a party with an interest in it being true.
>
> **Contested questions.** Where scholars or practitioners genuinely disagree, represent the disagreement rather than resolving it for me. Give me the strongest version of each position.
>
> **Style.** Be direct. Skip preamble, flattery, and summaries of what I just asked. Use plain language. If my question is ambiguous, ask rather than guessing.

## What this does and does not do

**It reduces fabrication. It does not eliminate it.**

These tools generate text by predicting what should come next, not by retrieving verified facts. That is why they invent sources, and it is a property of the technology rather than a defect awaiting a patch. Instructions change the tool's defaults; they do not change what it fundamentally is.

So the obligation remains yours:

- Open every source and confirm it exists and says what the tool claims
- Verify every statistic against its original, not against a summary
- Check every quotation verbatim
- Confirm the jurisdiction and the policy status yourself
- Be able to defend every sentence you submit

In this course, **unverified or fabricated citations in submitted work are treated as an academic-integrity matter**, regardless of which tool produced them. Outside a classroom the stakes are different but not lower: a fabricated figure in a briefing note is something your principal may repeat on the record.

The tool is not the author. You are.
