# The tensions ledger

The course throughline, kept as a running log.

Almost every consequential decision about technology in government is a trade-off dressed as a technical question. Efficiency against equity. Speed against accountability. The value of joining up data against its capacity to harm the people inside it. Automating a decision against a citizen's right to a human being who can explain it.

AI does not resolve these tensions. It sharpens them, and it makes them move faster than the governance around them. The failure mode is not choosing badly — it is letting the tool quietly make the choice while everyone treats it as an implementation detail.

So each week, write three lines.

Your ledger feeds directly into Briefing Note 2 and your final project — it is where the trade-offs you will have to argue get worked out first.

---

## How to use it

- One row per teaching session.
- Keep entries short. A few sentences per cell is plenty; this should take two minutes, not twenty.
- **"Who bears the cost" is the most important column.** Be specific about *which* people or groups — that specificity is the whole discipline.
- Note where AI *sharpens* an existing tension rather than creating a new one. Most of these trade-offs predate the technology.
- Resist the urge to resolve it. The point is to see it clearly.

---

## How it relates to the diagnostic

Two instruments, deliberately different weights:

| | Tensions ledger | [Adaptive capacity diagnostic](./adaptive-capacity-diagnostic.md) |
|---|---|---|
| **What it is** | The course throughline | The course takeaway |
| **When** | Every teaching session | Seven sessions (3, 4, 5, 8, 9, 10, 13) |
| **Length** | Three lines | 300–400 words |
| **About** | The week's theme | A real institution you chose in Session 1 |

**In the seven diagnostic weeks, the diagnostic entry contains your ledger entry** — "the tension here" is one of its required fields, so there is no double work. The ledger runs on its own in Sessions 2, 6, 11, and 12.

---

## The three lines

| | |
|---|---|
| **What is gained** | Be concrete. "Efficiency" is not an answer — how much, for whom, measured how? |
| **What is risked** | Including the risk nobody in the room wants to raise. |
| **Who bears the cost** | Name actual people or groups. This is the line that gets skipped, and it is the one that matters. |

## Why it is worth the two minutes

- It converts a vague unease into a statement someone can argue with.
- It surfaces the distributional question — who pays — which technical discussions reliably bury.
- It gives you a defensible position months later, when the decision is being reviewed and memories have moved.
- Written down, it is the beginning of a briefing note. Kept in your head, it is a feeling you cannot act on.

**The point is not to resolve the tension.** Most of these cannot be resolved. The point is to hold it visibly, so that a human being is accountable for the trade-off rather than a system making it by default.
