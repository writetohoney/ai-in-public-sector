# The Adaptive Capacity Diagnostic

**A working instrument, and the thing you take away from this course.**

Most of what you learn in a course about AI in government will date. The specific tools, the leading jurisdictions, the current guidance — all of it moves. This instrument is designed to outlast that. It assesses something slower: whether the institution you work in can *see, decide, absorb, and correct* at the speed a problem demands.

AI is the stress test, not the subject. It is well suited to the role because it arrives fast, is hard to see inside, concentrates decisions, and fails in unfamiliar ways — so it exposes weaknesses that slower technologies let institutions leave unaddressed. But the diagnostic works on whatever the next stress test turns out to be.

By the end of the course you will hold a completed assessment of a real institution. That is the takeaway.

---

## How it works

**Choose an institution in Session 1.** A department, agency, municipality, branch, or unit — ideally your own, or one you know well enough that evidence exists. It must be specific: "the federal government" is not assessable; "the benefits delivery unit I work in" is.

**Assess one dimension per week.** Seven sessions carry a dimension (see the table below). In each, you complete one entry: what evidence you would look at, what you actually found, the tension it carries, and one intervention that would strengthen it.

**One weekly entry, not two.** The tension each dimension carries is a built-in field, so this is the course's single recurring device — light enough to sustain, structured enough to accumulate into something you keep.

**Two rules.**

> **Specificity over judgment.** "We could do better on data governance" is not an assessment. "I asked three people where our client data lives and got three answers, and no one could produce an inventory" is.

> **Name what you could not see.** An honest assessment records its own blind spots. Outsiders cannot observe internal decision-making; insiders cannot observe their own assumptions. Both matter, and saying so is a strength.

## The seven dimensions

### 1 · Sensing
**Can it see what is coming, and tell a real claim from a sold one?**

*Look at:* Who is responsible for noticing? Where did the last three technology decisions originate — internally, or with a supplier? Is there anyone who can evaluate a capability claim independently? What happened the last time a vendor claim turned out to be overstated?

*Strength looks like:* claims tested before they are adopted; internal people who can read a technical proposal; awareness that arrives before the procurement does.

*Weakness looks like:* the institution learns about technologies from the people selling them; enthusiasm at the top with no independent evaluation beneath it.

*The tension:* moving early enough to matter versus moving on evidence rather than enthusiasm.

---

### 2 · Self-legibility
**Can it see itself?**

*Look at:* Could it produce an accurate inventory of its systems and data holdings? How long would that take, and who would have to be asked? Does anyone know its full dependency map? When something breaks, how long to find out why?

*Strength looks like:* current documentation; someone who can answer "what do we run and what depends on it" in a day rather than a quarter.

*Weakness looks like:* institutional knowledge held only in individuals; no one able to say what a system does or who maintains it. Alberta's Velocity program began here on purpose — the province could not see its own code, and made it legible before changing anything.

*The tension:* the cost and discomfort of looking honestly versus the impossibility of adapting blind.

---

### 3 · Absorptive capacity
**Can it acquire new capability without becoming dependent on whoever supplied it?**

*Look at:* What share of technical capability sits with people it employs? Could it evaluate — or replace — its largest supplier? Does knowledge from contracts stay when the contractors leave? What does its procurement process reward?

*Strength looks like:* buying that builds internal capability; contracts that transfer knowledge; the ability to walk away.

*Weakness looks like:* critical systems no employee understands; procurement optimized for compliance rather than capability; vendor lock-in described as partnership.

*The tension:* the speed and expertise of buying versus the capability and control of building.

---

### 4 · Structural flexibility
**Can it move money, people, and rules at the speed a problem demands?**

*Look at:* How long from "we should do this" to money actually moving? What is the fastest it has ever reallocated staff? Which constraints are statutory, and which are habit that everyone believes is statutory? Is funding attached to projects or to products?

*Strength looks like:* reallocation within a cycle; someone who knows which rules can actually flex.

*Weakness looks like:* every constraint individually defensible and collectively immobilizing — the cascade of rigidity.

*The tension:* the controls that make spending accountable versus the flexibility that makes response possible.

---

### 5 · Acting under uncertainty
**Can it decide and experiment before it has full information, without catastrophe?**

*Look at:* When did it last run something genuinely allowed to fail? What happened to the people involved? Are pilots designed to learn, or designed to succeed? How are decisions made when the evidence is incomplete — and who carries the risk?

*Strength looks like:* small reversible experiments; failures that produce learning rather than blame; decisions made at the level with the most information.

*Weakness looks like:* pilots that always work; no decision without certainty that never arrives; agile vocabulary over waterfall practice.

*The tension:* deciding fast enough to matter versus deciding accountably — remembering that not deciding is also a decision with costs.

---

### 6 · Feedback and correction
**Can it learn that it is wrong — from the people affected — and change course?**

*Look at:* How does it find out it has harmed someone? How long does that take? What does a person have to do to contest a decision, and how many give up? Has a complaint ever actually changed a system? Is there a route that does not require a journalist?

*Strength looks like:* harm detected internally and early; contestation that is genuinely accessible; evidence of decisions reversed because someone objected.

*Weakness looks like:* burden as a feedback-suppression mechanism — when contesting is hard enough, the institution stops hearing that it is wrong. The A-level algorithm is the case where feedback arrived only as public outcry, the most expensive form.

*The tension:* the efficiency of automating a decision versus the citizen's right to a human who can explain it and be argued with.

---

### 7 · Legitimacy
**Can it change without losing the public trust that makes it worth preserving?**

*Look at:* Would it survive its current technology decisions being reported accurately? Does it disclose where automated systems shape decisions? Are equity and impact assessments used as instruments or as paperwork? What would it have to do for the public to stop accepting its decisions — and how close is it?

*Strength looks like:* transparency that precedes the story; assessment that changes designs rather than documenting them.

*Weakness looks like:* compliance artefacts produced after decisions are made; a gap between what the institution says it does and what it does.

*The tension:* moving fast enough to remain capable versus moving in a way the public will continue to authorize.

---

## The entry template

One per dimension, roughly 300–400 words.

| | |
|---|---|
| **Dimension** | |
| **Evidence I looked at** | What you actually examined — documents, conversations, observations |
| **What I found** | Be specific. Quote what people said. Note what you could not obtain. |
| **What I could not see** | The honest limits of your vantage point |
| **The tension here** | What is gained, what is risked, and who bears the cost |
| **One intervention** | The single most useful thing that would strengthen this dimension |

---

## Completing the assessment

In Session 14, bring the seven entries together:

1. **The two or three dimensions that most constrain adaptation** in your institution — and your evidence for that ranking.
2. **Sequenced interventions.** What first, and why that order? Strengthening self-legibility usually precedes everything; legitimacy work is hardest to retrofit after a failure.
3. **Return to your Session 1 intuition.** What did you get wrong, and what changed your mind? This is often the most valuable part.

**Optional capstone track.** Students who want to go further may submit the completed adaptive capacity assessment as their final project in place of the policy memo or prototype, expanded to ~4,000 words with full evidence and a sequenced intervention plan.

## Two honest caveats

**This is a teaching heuristic, not an established framework.** The seven dimensions are a synthesis built for this course, drawing on adjacent scholarship — absorptive capacity (Cohen & Levinthal), dynamic capabilities, organizational resilience, and state capacity — without being a formal application of any of them. Use it as a lens that makes an institution legible, and say so if you cite it. If you find a dimension that does not fit your institution, that is information about the framework, not a failure of your assessment.

**A diagnostic that hardens into a maturity model has become the problem it was meant to detect.** Scores, ratings, and box-ticking are the failure mode here. There are deliberately no numbers in this instrument. The point is a clearer account of a specific institution, not a grade.

And it is worth holding the strongest objection: Vertesi, boyd, Taylor and Shestakofsky argue that institutional reform can itself function as a decoy — an institution could strengthen every dimension here while the actual locus of power sits entirely elsewhere. That argument is assigned in Session 12. A course that teaches this instrument should be able to sit with the possibility that the instrument is insufficient.
