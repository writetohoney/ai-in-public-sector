# AI for Political Staffers — An Intensive

### Cut through the hype, use it without getting burned, and advise your principal

**Format:** 3 half-days (flexes to 2 or 3 full days — see [Running it in other formats](#running-it-in-other-formats)) · **Audience:** ministerial and political staff, legislative and caucus staff, campaign and party staff · **Prerequisite:** none — no technical background required

---

> **Adaptation note.** This is a compressed, audience-adapted version of the *AI in the Public Sector* graduate course, whose architecture is in turn modelled on *Applied AI Systems and Governance* by Jaxson Khan, University of Toronto Munk School. It keeps that course's throughline — *governing AI well means holding tensions, not resolving them away* — but rebuilds it for people who advise elected officials and work on the news cycle's clock. See [`../LICENSE.md`](../LICENSE.md).

## Why this, and why it's different from the staff-college version

Political staffers don't spend their days procuring models or writing algorithmic impact assessments. They brief a principal, turn work around in hours, live in issues management and communications, and carry real risk — a leak, a botched line, a principal repeating something an AI made up. So this intensive answers three staffer questions, one per half-day:

1. **Can I trust this?** — enough fluency to pressure-test AI claims (your side's and your opponents')
2. **Can I use it without getting burned?** — a safe personal AI workflow, and the guardrails
3. **How do I advise my principal?** — the governance landscape, the tensions, and how to position

## What you'll leave with

- Enough fluency to tell an AI claim that's real from one that's hype
- A safe, repeatable personal AI workflow, plus the course's anti-fabrication custom instructions
- A political risk playbook for government AI — how these systems become scandals, and how not to
- A one-page map of the governance landscape (Taiwan, Alberta, EU, US, OECD) and how to position your principal for or against
- A rapid-response briefing *you drafted* in the room, red-teamed by your peers

## Pre-work (about 30 minutes)

- Read: [Large Language Models, explained](https://www.understandingai.org/p/large-language-models-explained-with) — Lee & Trott (skim for the mental model, not the detail)
- Skim one AI-in-government story that became a political problem — e.g. the [UK A-level grading fiasco](https://blogs.lse.ac.uk/impactofsocialsciences/2020/08/26/fk-the-algorithm-what-the-world-can-learn-from-the-uks-a-level-grading-fiasco/) or the ArriveCAN Auditor General report — and come ready to say *what made it political*.

## Half-Day 1 — Cut Through the Hype

**Goal:** leave able to interrogate any AI claim that crosses your desk.

**9:00 – 9:20 · Welcome & framing.** Why AI matters for staffers specifically. The two failure modes we avoid all week: *hype* (repeating vendor and headline claims uncritically) and *reflexive fear* (dismissing it and getting blindsided).

**9:20 – 10:10 · How AI actually works, fast.** Machine learning and large language models in plain language — what they do, what "training" gives them, and why they confidently make things up ("hallucinate"). No code. The point: you can't pressure-test a claim about a technology you treat as magic.

**10:10 – 10:55 · What AI is for in government — and the limits of data.** The genuine public-sector uses versus the hype. Why a tool that dazzles in a demo fails on contact with messy data and politics. How to read a vendor pitch or a rival's announcement skeptically.

**10:55 – 11:10 · Break.**

**11:10 – 12:00 · The political risk landscape.** The distinctly *political* AI risks: deepfakes and synthetic media aimed at your principal, disinformation and election integrity, and the anatomy of an AI scandal — ArriveCAN, Robodebt, the A-level algorithm, the Dutch childcare-benefits affair. What these failures have in common, and who ends up paying (usually the most vulnerable, then the minister).

**12:00 – 12:30 · Exercise — "Spot the hype, spot the risk."** In pairs, work through a set of real AI announcements and claims. Which are credible? Which would you want verified before your principal repeats them? Where's the buried political risk? Debrief.

## Half-Day 2 — Use It Without Getting Burned

**Goal:** leave with a personal AI workflow you can use Monday, and the guardrails to keep you and your principal safe.

**9:00 – 9:10 · Recap.**

**9:10 – 10:20 · Hands-on lab — AI for the staffer's actual work.** A working session using the course [custom instructions](../instructions/student-research-custom-instructions.md). We'll run real staffer tasks: fast background research and synthesis, first-draft briefing notes, Question Period / debate prep and anticipated-Q&A, constituent and stakeholder correspondence, and speech and messaging drafts. Emphasis on *drafting and pressure-testing*, always human-finished.

**10:20 – 10:35 · Break.**

**10:35 – 11:20 · The guardrails — how not to get burned.** The staffer-specific risks and how to manage them:
- **Verify everything.** AI fabricates citations, statistics, and quotes. Nothing your principal repeats should be unverified. (This is what the custom instructions are for.)
- **Confidentiality and leaks.** Don't put cabinet confidences, personal data, or sensitive political material into consumer AI tools; know your office's approved-tools rules.
- **Records and access-to-information.** Prompts and outputs may be records; assume they could surface.
- **Disclosure and attribution.** When AI-assisted work is fine, and the danger of a principal unknowingly using AI-invented material.
- **The front-page test.** Would you be comfortable if this specific use were reported? If not, don't.

**11:20 – 12:20 · Governance & positioning.** The governance landscape in one sitting, built around the course's central tension — *deliver first vs. govern first*:
- **Alberta's Velocity White Papers** — bold, open-sourced, efficiency-and-scale-first modernization. Genuinely impressive on legacy tech debt; lighter on rights, accountability, and contestability.
- **Taiwan** — institution-and-rights-first: a framework AI Basic Act, data protection as the enforcement backbone, a sovereign training corpus, copyright as the front line.
- The **EU / US / OECD** frame in a page.
How to help your principal take a *defensible* position — championing efficiency without owning the next scandal, or critiquing a program without sounding anti-technology.

**12:20 – 12:30 · Debrief.**

---

## Half-Day 3 — Advise Your Principal

**Goal:** apply everything under time pressure, the way the job actually feels.

**9:00 – 9:15 · Set up the clinic.** Teams pick a scenario.

**9:15 – 10:30 · Rapid-response clinic.** Each team takes one scenario and produces a short principal-ready package: the facts to confirm, a risk assessment, recommended course of action, and holding/attack lines.

- **Scenario A — Issues management.** A government AI system (say, benefits eligibility) is reported to have wrongly cut people off. Your minister faces media today and Question Period tomorrow. Pause, review, or defend — and what do you say now?
- **Scenario B — Policy launch.** Your government wants to announce a bold, Alberta-style AI-efficiency initiative. Draft the framing, the rollout guardrails you insist on so it doesn't become Scenario A, the risk register, and the attacks you expect with rebuttals.
- **Scenario C — Opposition critique.** You work for the opposition; the government just announced an AI program or a large AI procurement. Build the critique, the committee/QP questions, and the tensions to press (rights, accountability, cost, vendor dependence) — while staying credible.

**10:30 – 10:45 · Break.**

**10:45 – 11:45 · Present & red-team.** Each team presents; the others play the hostile press gallery, the opposition, or the minister's skeptical chief of staff. This is where the tensions get stress-tested.

**11:45 – 12:30 · Synthesis & takeaways.** Build your personal **staffer AI checklist** and **AI watch list** — the questions to ask before your principal touches an AI issue, and the signals that a government AI story is about to break. Close.

## Running it in other formats

- **3 half-days (default).** As above — one self-contained module per half-day.
- **2 full days.** Day 1 = Half-Days 1 + 2. Day 2 = Half-Day 3, expanded with a second scenario round and a guest speaker (a former ministerial chief of staff, a press-gallery journalist, or an elections-integrity official).
- **3 full days.** Add an afternoon deep-dive lab each day: (1) a hands-on disinformation/deepfake-spotting session; (2) build-and-critique a simple policy assistant so staff feel what these tools can and can't do; (3) a comparative-governance deep dive (Taiwan vs. Alberta vs. EU) with a guest.

---

## Facilitator notes

- **Guest speakers** appear under the Chatham House Rule — essential for candid political-staff discussion.
- **Tech setup.** Participants need laptops and access to an approved AI tool for the Half-Day 2 lab; load the custom instructions in advance.
- **Keep it non-partisan in the room.** The scenarios deliberately include government and opposition roles so the skills transfer across any principal.
- **Materials to bring:** the [custom instructions](../instructions/student-research-custom-instructions.md), printed scenario cards, and the reading shortlist below.

## Reading shortlist (curated for staffers)

- [Large Language Models, explained](https://www.understandingai.org/p/large-language-models-explained-with) — Lee & Trott *(fluency)*
- [The UK A-level grading fiasco](https://blogs.lse.ac.uk/impactofsocialsciences/2020/08/26/fk-the-algorithm-what-the-world-can-learn-from-the-uks-a-level-grading-fiasco/) — Kolkman *(anatomy of an AI scandal)*
- [Guide on the use of generative AI](https://www.canada.ca/en/government/system/digital-government/digital-government-innovations/responsible-use-ai/guide-use-generative-ai.html) — Government of Canada *(safe use, disclosure)*
- [The Velocity White Papers](https://thevelocitywhitepapers.com/) — Government of Alberta *(deliver-first)*
- [Taiwan's Emerging AI Governance](https://www.nbr.org/publication/taiwans-emerging-ai-governance-building-from-the-basic-act-to-the-hard-questions-of-data-and-copyright/) — Huang, NBR *(govern-first)*
- [AI Meets the Cascade of Rigidity](https://www.digitalistpapers.com/essays/ai-meets-the-cascade-of-rigidity) — Pahlka *(why government AI is hard)*

*Because AI and its politics move fast, refresh the cases and the governance items each time this runs.*
